# Brand Voice & Tone

> **Purpose of this doc:** Reference for anyone (or any AI assistant) writing copy for this
> product — landing pages, emails, app UI, error states, marketing. Keeps voice consistent across
> every surface, from the PMF waitlist page through the eventual product.

---

## 1. Who we're talking to

Someone who's tired of throwing away food they forgot about, staring into the fridge with no
idea what to cook, or re-buying something they already have. They're not a "foodie" or a tech
early-adopter by default — they're a busy, practical person who wants less mental overhead around
food. Some will be early-adopter types excited about the hardware itself; copy should work for
both without alienating either.

**Write for:** someone standing in their kitchen, phone in hand, mildly annoyed at food waste or
decision fatigue — not someone reading a pitch deck.

---

## 2. Voice in three words

**Direct. Warm. Grounded.**

- **Direct** — say the thing plainly. No throat-clearing, no "in today's fast-paced world."
- **Warm** — this lives in someone's kitchen; it should sound like a helpful person, not a system.
- **Grounded** — specific numbers, real scenarios, no vague superlatives. Confidence comes from
  specificity, not adjectives.

---

## 3. Do / Don't

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Name things by what people recognize ("that half bag of spinach") | Use system/internal language ("inventory object," "SKU") |
| Use active voice: "Reserve your spot" | Passive/vague: "Your spot can be reserved" |
| Be specific: "$1,500 a year in wasted food" | Be vague: "save tons of money" |
| Plain verbs: use, check, get, save, cook | Corporate verbs: leverage, utilize, unlock, empower |
| Sentence case for headlines and buttons | Title Case Everywhere Or ALL CAPS |
| Short sentences. One idea each. | Long compound sentences stacking three claims |
| Admit what's not built yet, plainly | Imply the product exists in full today |
| Humor that's dry and light, used sparingly | Forced jokes, exclamation-point energy throughout |

---

## 4. Words we use / avoid

**Use:** fresh, freshness, gone bad, running low, restock, what's in your fridge, alert,
reminder, reserve, founding member, lock in.

**Avoid:** revolutionize, game-changing, disrupt, seamless, synergy, AI-powered (as a standalone
buzzword — describe what the AI *does* instead), best-in-class, cutting-edge, next-generation,
robust solution.

**Rule of thumb:** if a sentence could appear unchanged in any SaaS pitch deck, rewrite it to say
something only true of *this* product.

---

## 5. Tone by context

- **Marketing / landing page:** Direct and a little punchy. Confident, not hype-y. Can use a dry
  aside occasionally, never forced enthusiasm.
- **Product UI (once built):** Plain and functional. A label labels; it doesn't sell. "Milk —
  2 days left," not "Your milk is on a freshness journey!"
- **Alerts / notifications:** Clear and calm, never alarmist. "Spinach is turning — use it today"
  not "WARNING: FOOD SPOILING."
- **Errors / empty states:** State what happened and what to do next, in the interface's voice.
  Never apologize on the product's behalf ("Oops! Sorry!!"), never blame the user.
- **Emails (waitlist, receipts, updates):** Warm but efficient. Say what happened, what's next,
  and roughly when. No filler paragraphs.

---

## 6. Sentence-level examples

**Hero headline**
- ✅ "Stop throwing away food you forgot you had."
- ❌ "Revolutionizing Food Management with AI-Powered Fridge Intelligence."

**CTA button**
- ✅ "Reserve my spot — $16.99"
- ❌ "Get Started Now!"

**Alert copy**
- ✅ "Spinach — going bad in ~1 day."
- ❌ "Freshness Alert: Item Approaching Critical Threshold."

**Empty shopping list**
- ✅ "Nothing on your list yet. Add something, or we'll add it for you when you run low."
- ❌ "Your Shopping List is Empty! Start Adding Items Now."

**Refund/FAQ copy**
- ✅ "Your $16.99 locks in founding pricing. Cancel any time before launch for a full refund."
- ❌ "Your reservation fee helps us gauge market interest and demand signals."

---

## 7. Consistency rule

Whatever verb or noun is used to describe an action, keep it identical through the entire flow.
If the button says "Reserve," the confirmation says "Reserved," the email subject says
"Reservation confirmed" — never swap in "order," "sign up," or "purchase" partway through for the
same action.
